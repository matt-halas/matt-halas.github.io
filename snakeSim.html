Building a Neural Network to Play Snake

I have been wanting to build a bot that could play snake for a few years now, and a bout of motivation combined with a generous work schedule finally got me started on it. Although a neural network was probably overkill for a game like this, I wanted to create my own from scratch. A handmade algorithm with instruction sets probably would have been faster and better at the game, but the simple environment was a great place to try my hand at my own neural network.

Building the Game

Before working on anything related to the bot, I built up a game with a GUI that a human (I) could play. I used a tkinter GUI for the game display and control.

Building the Bot

Inputs

The first step was to give the snake eyes. The inverse distance between the front of the snake to an obstacle (wall or snake) or food was used as the input to the neural network. These inputs are relative to the direction of the snake, giving a left, right, and forward signal for obstacles and food, for a total of six inputs. The signal is stronger (gives a larger number) the closer the head of the snake is to an obstacle or piece of food.

Brain (Neural Network)

The brain takes the six inputs, passes them through the neural network, and spits out an output. Since I tried to do this with as little help from the internet as possible, neural network theory and best practices were thrown out of the window in favor of simplicity. In general, the network consists of input coefficients, layer coefficients, and output coefficients. I arbitrarily chose to use two intermediate layers with four neurons per layer. The neurons in each intermediate layer are activated if their resulting sum is greater than 0. The output is one-hot encoded for three actions; turn left, turn right, or continue straight, with the highest output being set to 1, and the other two being set to 0.

Below is an example of the calculation used to take the input and calculate the first layer of neurons. The resulting 4x1 matrix is converted to 1s and 0s based on if the resultant number is positive or negative to represent the neurons "firing" or not. F represents food signals, O represents obstacle signals, and the subscripts l, f, and r represent left, forward, and right respectively.

$ \begin{bmatrix} F_l \\ F_f \\ F_r \\ O_l \\ O_f \\ O_r \end{bmatrix} \; \times \; \begin{bmatrix} x_{1,1} & x_{1,2} & . & . & & \\ x_{2,1} & . & & & & \\ . & & . & & & \\ . & & & . & & \end{bmatrix} \; = \; \begin{bmatrix} F_l x_{1,1} + F_f x_{1,2} + F_r x_{1,3} + O_l x_{1,4} + O_f x_{1,5} + O_r x_{1,6} \\ ... \\ ... \\ ... \end{bmatrix} $

All coefficients were initially randomly generated, with a mutation function built in to modify coefficients for subsequent generations. The mutation function modifies each coefficeint by adding a randomly generated value from a normal distribution with a mean of 0 and a standard deviation that could be altered as the training went on. The standard deviation of the mutation is referred to as the "mutation intensity".

Scoring

Before training could start, a method to compare the success of each brain was needed. Initially the bot was scored strictly based on how many pieces of food it collected. This led to some fast training, but the resulting bots played the game inefficiently and only turned in one direction to reach the food. To spice things up, a time element was introduced to the score. Score was subtracted for every step taken to get to a food, with a maximum of 25 points and a minimum of 1 point.

Building an army of bots

To get any measure of success from randomly generated coefficients, a lot of brains needed to be created. Each brain was initially given 10 attempts, with their final score being the average of those tries. After simulating every brain in a given generation, the top ten were saved and mutated to create the next generation. The rest were pruned. Usefulness is survival. See the top 10 brains in the first generation play below.

Even if you're not good, try enough times and you might succeed. Initially, to prevent infinite length games, the brain lost if it took too many steps between collecting food. This timeout length increased as the bots got better.

The top ten brains of the current generation are used as the starting point for the following generation. Ten copies of each brain (for a total of 100) are created and mutated at a specified intensity. The intensity started out high and was manually tapered off to fine tune behaviour and the bots got better.

Training

With all the architecture in place for a randomly created bot to play the game, get it's scores, and create the next generation from the best of the current generation, I started the training process. Instead of having the bots play the game through the GUI, while training, they ran in a simulated game that I couldn't view. The training wasn't very methodical. I altered some parameters such as the mutation intensity, the score loss on each step, the number of games each bot played, and the length until timeout every 25 generations or so, as I saw fit.

After 125 generations, I was happy with the performance of the bots, so I ended the training there. Below is a video of the top bot of the 125th generation playing the game. (Please disregard the Stereolab in the background)

Results

I'm happy with how the bot turned out given how basic the neural network was. I'm sure that with more training, the average score could be increased a little more before reaching a flatter plateau. More improvement would probably come from including more information in the input, adjustments to how the bot is scored, or increasing starting length as the generations progress to increase the initial difficulty. The bot seems to struggle with boxing itself in once it reaches a certain length, which is not surprising given how little information is given in the inputs to the neural network.
